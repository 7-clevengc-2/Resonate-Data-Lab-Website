# Introduction
Members from our Resonate Data Lab volunteered for the Confederate Tribes of the Umatilla Indian Reservation’s (CTUIR) summer camp from June 23 to June 27, 2025, working alongside students ranging from 2nd grade to 5th grade.  Our team knows that sound has deep ties to identity, perception, and connection, but sound is generally not woven into education.  Over the five days at the summer camp, our team’s goal was to help students deepen their relationship with sound and empower their learning by showing them that there is not a singular way to learn.  Our lessons were structured around listening, recording, creating, and analyzing sounds, where students took on the roles of *Sound Detectives*, *Sound Collectors*, *Sound Designers*, *Sound Problem Solvers*, and *Sound Builders*.
Our team also learned a lot from this summer camp, helping us revise our lesson plans for the upcoming *Your Life in Sound Class* at Whitman College in the Fall semester.  The sections below will go into more detail about our lesson plans from Monday to Friday, our challenges and successes, what we learned from teaching each day, and how to recreate the lessons for future students.

# Monday: Intro to Sounds & Mystery Sound Bag
Someone once told me that the true measure of knowledge is the ability to pass it on to others. Together as a lab, priding ourselves in this beautiful pursuit of what it means to think and reflect with sound, took on the opportunity to conduct a week-long STEM summer course at CTUIR summer camp with grades 3-5.

I had the opportunity to design the activity for Day 1, which was titled "Becoming Sound Detectives." The students participating in our activity were asked to use their creative abilities to create sounds from mysterious objects and also use their ears to describe and guess the sounds of the objects.
Grades 2–5 students became “Sound Detectives” in a 45-minute session exploring how we use sound to communicate, imagine, and understand the world. After warming up with a silent listening activity and sound-based introductions, students teamed up for a blindfolded guessing game using mystery sound bags. They practiced focused listening, described unfamiliar sounds, and collaborated to identify hidden objects. Through playful challenges and group reflection, students discovered how sound can spark curiosity and connection no sight required. This engaging session kicked off a week of sensory exploration, creativity, and deep listening. 
For our planning, we spent time as a team, brainstorming and prototyping this acidity. We made lesson plans, draft after draft, and kept on refining them until we had a working prototype.
We later searched for nosy and mysterious objects that we placed into paper bags and randomly labeled them. We also created blindfolds to aid in the guessing process.

Our sole objective was to design a fun, immersive introductory activity for this week that would be filled with exploration of sound and what it has to offer. I believe this activity was a great launching pad for the remainder of the week. We got to access the attitudes of the students and came back each day ready to match their desire to learn and explore with sound.

Overall, I think the day went well. The kids brought so much energy and excitement to the space, and it was amazing to see how engaged they were, especially during the sound guessing rounds. Sarena did note that their attention began to drop after a couple of turns, which made sense, as the activity moved fairly quickly and some students were eager for more variety. I was honestly nervous about how the session would go, but the activity was not only well-received by the students, it also got great feedback from my fellow facilitators, which was incredibly reassuring.
One thing I’d do differently next time is structure the debrief and discussion portion in a more simplified, child-friendly way. While the kids had great thoughts to share, I realized the language we use to guide reflection matters. I'd also prepare a few extra follow-up games or sound challenges to pivot to when energy drops, something we had to improvise today by having students swap bags, which turned out to be a great spur-of-the-moment solution.
It was a strong start to our week of sound exploration, and I’m already thinking of ways to keep the momentum going. Seeing the students so immersed in listening, imagining, and creating sounds reminded me how powerful simple sensory play can be.

# Thursday: Sounds and Numbers

For Thursday’s lesson, our goal was to guide our students to use their awareness of sound, which they had developed in our previous lessons, to solve problems using sounds.  We wanted students to come away from our lesson with:

- The ability to perceive, describe, and distinguish between different qualities of sound
- The ability to map sound variables to quantitative information
- The ability to convey quantitative information using sound
- An awareness of how sonic representations of data compare to visual representations of data

Some considerations we had to make while creating this lesson plan were differences in our students’ knowledge bases, since our age group ranged from 2nd to 5th graders, and many students were catching up on their general education.  Therefore, none of our activities require any mathematical calculations beyond comparing quantities and don’t use terminology related to graphs.

In our first activity, students rotated through four sound stations that tasked them with solving a problem relating to the station’s sound variable (pitch, timbre, tempo, and direction).  The facilitators at each station would give a simple description of the sound variable and then introduce the word problem along with accompanying audio clips students would need to solve the problem.  Our team noticed that students were able to use the different sound qualities to reason through the word problems.  However, the students seemed particularly antsy and had some difficulty focusing on the task at hand.  The acoustics of the room the stations were in also may have impacted our students’ focus, since the room was quite noisy even though the stations were decently far apart, making it challenging for students to listen when facilitators played the sounds.  In the future, ensuring that the stations are in a quiet environment and spaced out from each other may help students focus better on solving the problems.  Specifically for the direction station, having a smaller group of students at the station seems necessary since the students’ placements affected the volume of the sounds they heard when the facilitator played sounds in different directions.  Also, students liked being able to play with and move the cut-out visuals at each station to form their answers. Encouraging this aspect of play while problem-solving could help future students remain interested in the activity.

For our second activity, we introduced students to sonifications or representations of data through sound.  We tasked students with representing the heights of different characters by using a sound quality of their choosing.  Students were allowed to use their voices or tools from Chrome Music Lab to create these representations.  Three different graphs of aliens, people, and zoo animals (with increasing difficulty) were provided for the activity. Our students seemed to enjoy the creative and imaginative aspects of this activity.  Many students also seemed to rely on their intuition and past experiences with sound to create their sonic graphs.  For example, students who chose to represent height using pitch decided that smaller characters would have a higher-pitched voice while taller characters would have a lower-pitched voice.  Their chosen mapping of pitch to height is often representative of reality (for example, a mouse has a high-pitched squeak while a bear has a deep roar).  If we had more time for this activity, we would like students to try unconventional mappings of the sound variables to height, such as representing smaller characters with low pitches.

In conclusion, students performed well with both activities. The most challenging part of this lesson was keeping students engaged.  In the future, creating a quiet environment and emphasizing aspects of play could help students remain focused on the activity.

# Friday: Making Instruments

Friday marked our final day with the CTUIR kids, wrapping up a week full of exploration, creativity, and learning about sound. Over the past few days, we introduced the children, ages 8 to 11, to the sounds in their surroundings and guided them in discovering that sound is produced by vibrations. We showed them how pitch changes based on tension and length, and how everyday materials can be used to make sound-based tools and instruments. This last session tied everything together with a fun and expressive craft activity.

We began by demonstrating a few simple instruments we had made beforehand: a rubber band guitar using a small cardboard box, and a flute crafted from straws cut at different lengths. These models helped us show how sound can change depending on what materials you use and how you shape them. When we asked the kids if they played any instruments or noticed interesting sounds in their daily life, they were mostly quiet at first. But once we moved into the hands-on portion, their excitement and creativity took over. We provided a wide range of materials, including tissue boxes, cardboard tubes, rubber bands of different thicknesses, lolly sticks, glue, scissors, buttons, and colorful paper. After dividing the group into smaller teams, each child began building and decorating their own instrument. Some focused on structure, others on decoration, and many did both. What stood out was the way they began personalizing their creations. One guitar was named Cardi B, linking the instrument to a singer the student admired. Others gave their instruments names and imagined their sounds as voices or characters. It was a clear sign that they were connecting to the activity not just through science, but through self-expression.

At the end of the session, we held a mini performance where everyone had the chance to show off their instruments and the sounds they created. Some strummed confidently, while others talked about how they decorated their guitars and what made them unique. Throughout the activity, we heard them casually use concepts we had introduced earlier in the week, like how tighter rubber bands made higher-pitched sounds. Even those who hadn’t spoken much at the start of the week were eager to share by the end. This session, and the week as a whole, reminded us that learning doesn’t always have to begin with facts and definitions. Sometimes, it begins with play, with creating something new from familiar materials, and with the freedom to explore without pressure. These kids went from listening to experimenting, from observing to making, and from being hesitant to expressing themselves with pride. Watching that transformation, and hearing the joy in the sounds they created, was the most rewarding part of the experience.
